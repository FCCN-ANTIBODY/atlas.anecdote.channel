# What the Atlas exposes — the public jurisdiction page, the ternary, the query language

> Status: **raw idea capture**, not a spec. **Transplanted** from the design trail in
> [`tell.anecdote.channel/notes/boundary-declaration.md`](https://github.com/FCCN-ANTIBODY/tell.anecdote.channel/blob/main/notes/boundary-declaration.md)
> (sessions 4–5) because these threads are about **exposure**, which is the **Atlas's** side:
> a Tell (and the anecdote layer) *mints*; the **Atlas is what exposes what anything mints for public
> view.** Nothing here is decided or built. Cross-read with the boundary note for the Tell-side
> mechanics (how a Tell declares/mints a boundary).

## The frame: anecdotes are minted below; the Atlas exposes them

A Tell **mints** founding anecdotes (its boundary among them); members mint anecdotes within a Tell's
constitution. **None of that is public until something routes it.** The **Atlas is the router and the
public face** — "the side that does exposure of what anything mints, for public view." This is already
the Atlas's stated self: a **directory** that *reflects what the piles consent to show, faithfully, and
**without flattering popularity*** (CONSTITUTION). So everything below is a development of "what,
exactly, does the public see when it calls up a jurisdiction — and how is it ordered without an oracle."

## The truly public page says *what they say*, not the data that moves

The load-bearing clarification. When someone calls up a jurisdiction's **truly public page**, it is
**not exposing all the data moving around** in sight of these nodes. It **merely says what they say** —
the **opinions**, which are public **by nature**. Two distinct layers:

- **Public, by nature — "what they say."** The minted anecdotes/opinions. This is what the jurisdiction
  page shows. Public because an opinion offered to be discovered *is* a public act (you minted it to
  socialize it; see below).
- **Licensed, consentful, behind the scenes — "the data that moves."** The actual data flowing on the
  network — **how we answer and talk to each other** — does move, but it is **not** on the public page.
  *"What we say can be licensed"*: if someone wants to care about our opinions, they can **license
  consentfully** how those opinions were answered and discussed. The page never exposes that; it is a
  separate, consent-gated channel.

This is not a new privacy promise — it is the Atlas's existing law read precisely: *"Any terms a pile
places with its data ride with it through me. I honor them, I do not strip them, and I release nothing a
pile's own terms forbid"* and *"I publish coarse standing, never raw per-respondent counts."* The page =
**coarse public opinion**; the data = **terms-bound, licensable**. The split makes a fully public
jurisdiction page **safe**: there is nothing on it to suppress, because it carries opinions, not the
record of who said what to whom.

## The jurisdiction page is the real-time democracy

No node *owns* a jurisdiction (no civic-node or workspace is authoritative over it), so what the page
shows is **necessarily the aggregation the Atlas provides** — the recorded "give up the
view-from-nowhere; every aggregate is lens-relative" decision, read as a **rendering** fact.

Minting makes every tier legible the same way, **up to as big as a state**:

- **Tells mint opinions** (founding anecdotes — boundaries and the rest).
- The Atlas holds the **phantom shape** that *contains* those Tells — ownerless, emergent from
  convergence of boundary claims — and **that shape *is* "your state."** The state never speaks; it is
  the **shape the speaking Tells fall inside.**
- So the page **literally is the real-time democracy of what those Tells are saying** — the **live
  aggregate of the anecdotes minted within the phantom boundary**, not a report *about* the jurisdiction
  from anyone who owns it. Same render rule at every tier (neighborhood → state); "state" is just the
  largest phantom shape drawn so far.

## Elevate without a trending algorithm

Hard constraint: **no opaque trending ranker** decides what rises. The Atlas already swore it —
*reflect… **without flattering popularity***. The raw material is the **"defusing the default"**
disciplines (from the boundary trail):

- credibility shown **as data** (meter / attestation count / overlap %), never used to **anoint**;
- **perturb** position, don't **curve** it (list + occasional **on-device** order reversal — curving is
  itself editorial);
- **tier**, don't flatten (premium claims share the shuffled top; jokes/opt-in below the fold);
- **no unmarked norm** (every claim marked by attester + attributes — never "official/normal/default").

Open whether these + the edge/lens machinery (the **bisect stack**, the **consent ladder**) suffice to
render a satisfying "real-time democracy," or whether a missing **non-algorithmic** elevation primitive
is needed. The constraint is fixed: **convergence shown as data, never a trending oracle.**

## The ternary — show three voices, never two

A principle proposed for **enshrinement**: expose a contested thing as a **ternary**, not a binary —

1. what **anecdote** says,
2. what **those trying to speak for it** say (the establishment Tells — see below), and
3. perhaps what **recognized government** says,

— and the set may **scale with whose boundaries are in competition** at a contested spot. Why three
beats two:

- In a **binary they can hide**: a yes/no lets a position pass as the obvious one, and anyone who
  collapses to binary is **"always sharing turf with someone else who flipped the same coin."**
- With **three, you get the chance to care how you look next to something else** — a position becomes
  legible **by contrast** and cannot masquerade as the default. (The structural form of "no unmarked
  norm": a third voice denies the binary its hiding place.)
- **Open:** how the third voice (and the scaling-with-contestants) is chosen **without** re-introducing a
  privileged slot; and how the internally-plural establishment collapses into a single "voice 2."

**Why this is the point of anecdote at all (the motivation behind the ternary).** There is currently
**nothing for us to stand beside** — **not *us* alone, and not the governments that fail us by not
listening well.** **Companies are capturing that public layer along with the governments**, and their
**terms of service and privacy policies increasingly sell us out** — *"they don't even understand what
the alternative is; they barely understand where they came from in a binary."* anecdote is meant to be
the **third thing to stand beside.** (This is mission-level — it may deserve a top-level vision note of
its own, beyond this Atlas capture.)

## The establishment tier — state-wide Tells, acknowledged but not shown first

Some Tells are **implicitly Tells for the whole state** — occupied with **what the state should say**,
pointing at a jurisdiction shape up to Colorado-sized. What they say is **specifically trying to be
relevant**, and that intent is worth **acknowledging implicitly** — e.g. the Atlas can **mark the Tells
it points at a jurisdiction shape**. But:

- **Relevant-by-intent ≠ shown-first.** Even a "first" Tell only competes with what the Atlas is saying,
  and the Atlas's own utterance is **definitely new** relative to what's already said.
- This tier is, more or less, **the establishment** — and **even amongst themselves they are plural.**
  **How to aggregate even them into one voice is open.** (This is "voice 2" of the ternary.)

## Query the Atlas by the same label structure it lists its polls under

We want to **query Atlases with the same label structure the Atlas uses to list its polls.** That
keyword structure is meant to **cover all ideas** because it is **label-reducing** — it decomposes an
idea into its components — so there is a **unified language everything renders under, even as that
language gets better over time.**

- This is the same taxonomy the **bisect stack's "vertical list of concepts"** needs, and it is a
  **direction for the open `concept`-vocabulary question** on the Tell side: a **shared, reductive,
  evolving** label language — neither a free-for-all nor a frozen central taxonomy. (The risk to watch:
  a controlled vocabulary is itself a soft-power center; "reducing to components" + "improvable over
  time" is the hedge against freezing it.)

## Self-listed anecdotes — minting is social, the Atlas is the router

An Atlas (or a Tell) can **list its own anecdotes** — **not** ones promoted from below, but the ones
**it itself says** — and this **only matters if they intend to be discovered through an Atlas.** From
which:

- **Minting an anecdote is a social behavior.** A minted anecdote is **meant for someone else who isn't
  present**, so it needs a **router — and the router is the Atlas.** You mint to **socialize** it.
- **You can't control what your Atlas says, but you must agree to its constitution to be there at all —
  and you can leave for one that says something you like better, by design.** (The existing PR-as-consent
  registration gesture and the principal's right to leave, one tier up.)

## Open threads (let simmer)

- **Two-layer exposure** — design the boundary between the **public opinion page** and the
  **consentful data license**: what licensing "how we answer and talk to each other" concretely looks
  like, and how a page guarantees it shows only the opinion layer.
- **Real-time-democracy render** — where it lives (Atlas neutral-geometry/aggregate vs. Apex/edge
  curation), and the DNS angle (`state-name.anecdote.channel` handing out a jurisdiction's view).
- **Elevation without a ranking function** — do the defusing-the-default disciplines + edge lens
  suffice, or is a non-algorithmic primitive missing? Never resolve with a trending oracle.
- **The ternary** — how the third voice is chosen neutrally; how it scales with contestants; how the
  plural establishment becomes one voice.
- **Unified query/label language** — reductive, componential, improvable; reconcile with the Tell-side
  `concept` vocabulary; guard against the controlled-vocabulary soft-power center.
- **A Tell disagreeing with an Atlas's minted anecdotes** — if an Atlas mints, is its mint
  *constitutional* (binding) or *just anecdotes it offers*? (Carried from the boundary trail; the
  offered-anecdotes path keeps disagreement cheap, resolved by overlap within the Atlas's constitution.)
- **Promote the why-anecdote-exists thesis** to a top-level vision note — it is the mission, larger than
  this Atlas capture.
